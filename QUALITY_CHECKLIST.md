# Quality Checklist

Run this list on every draft before it leaves your desk. Each "no" gets fixed
and re-checked. Only when all sixteen are "yes" does the piece ship.

This is not a stylistic preference. The checklist is what separates a
publishable case study from marketing fiction.

---

## The sixteen points

**1. Customer's perspective.**
Is the story told from the user's seat, not from the vendor's? No "we"
arguments. No "our product enables…". The customer is the subject of every
sentence in the body text.

**2. Recognisable working reality.**
Is there a concrete working situation the reader can see themselves in? Not
abstract claims about an industry — a real day at this company.

**3. Benefit over feature.**
Does the benefit lead, with the feature in support? Not the other way around.
A feature only earns its place when paired with what it changes in daily work.

**4. The W-questions are covered.**
Who, what, why, how, when, where. If any is missing, the piece is incomplete.

**5. Every statement is sourced.**
No invented numbers, no invented quotes, no invented examples. Anything that
doesn't trace back to the interview, webinar, or website is out — or flagged
as `[NEEDS INPUT]` / `[EDITORIAL]`.

**6. Concrete improvements are visible.**
Where the customer mentioned numbers (minutes, hours, days, percent, count),
they appear in the text. Improvements are *shown*, not asserted in adjectives.

**7. The arc matches the type.**
Type 1 (new customer): Portrait → starting point → decision → solution →
outcomes. Type 2 (long-term): Portrait → journey so far → current topic →
modules in focus → benefit and outlook. No forced before/after on a
long-term customer.

**8. Taboo words appear only with proof.**
"Efficient", "seamless", "future-proof", "intuitive", "optimised", "powerful",
"significant", "flexible" and their cousins are either absent from the body
text or immediately followed by concrete evidence. (Inside verbatim quotes,
the customer's language stays untouched.)

**9. Every quote is fully attributed.**
Name, role, and company on every quote. No anonymous "a manager said". No
invented quotes.

**10. Exactly one pull quote.**
One especially pointed statement is selected as the prominent pull quote
(maximum two sentences). Not zero, not three.

**11. Module and product names are correct.**
Every module or product name comes from the glossary, the source verbatim, or
the vendor's official site. None are guessed.

**12. The text stands without images.**
No "as shown in the photo", no "see figure". The body text carries the story
on its own. Important examples are described in prose, not delegated to a
caption.

**13. Tone is alive, not brochure-like.**
Reportage register: an experienced colleague telling a peer what changed.
Not marketing copy. Sentence lengths vary. Verbs are concrete.

**14. Length is in range.**
Roughly 600–900 words of body text. Substantially shorter feels thin;
substantially longer loses the reader.

**15. Missing facts are marked, not filled.**
Gaps appear as `[NEEDS INPUT: …]` or `[EDITORIAL: …]`. They are never papered
over with plausible-sounding filler.

**16. Approval note is set; sensitive statements are flagged.**
The draft ends with: *"Draft — quotes and statements to be approved by the
customer before publication."* Anything sensitive about people, internals,
or third parties is explicitly marked as approval-required.

---

## How to run the checklist

Print or copy the sixteen lines next to your draft. Walk them in order. Don't
skip ones that "obviously pass" — those are the points most likely to be
missed in practice (it's usually point 8 or point 11 that catches things).

When you find a "no":
- Fix the specific spot.
- Re-read the affected paragraph in context.
- Re-check the same point.

When every point is "yes", attach the list to the draft as evidence of the
gate.

---

## Why this works

The checklist is not arbitrary. Each point neutralises a specific failure
mode that case studies fall into when the writer is in a hurry or the source
material is thin:

- Points 1–3, 13 prevent the piece from drifting into vendor marketing.
- Points 4–6, 15 prevent invention and ungrounded claims.
- Point 7 prevents the wrong shape (forced before/after).
- Point 8 catches lazy adjectives.
- Points 9–11 protect attribution.
- Point 12 ensures the text works on its own.
- Point 14 keeps it readable.
- Point 16 protects the customer relationship.

If your draft passes all sixteen, you can ship it with confidence. If it
doesn't, the failing points tell you exactly where to look.
