# Methodology

How to turn a customer interview into a published case study.

This document is tool-agnostic. It describes the editorial method — how the
story is shaped, what counts as truth, how quotes are handled, how the draft is
checked before it leaves your desk. Whether you write the draft yourself, hand
it to an LLM, or do something in between, the rules below are what makes the
output trustworthy.

---

## What a case study is — and what it is not

A customer case study is **service journalism from the user's point of view**.
It answers the classic reporter's questions — *who, what, why, how, when,
where* — and helps a prospective buyer decide whether the story applies to
their own situation.

It is structured around three movements:

1. **Starting point** — the customer's situation before, the pain.
2. **Solution** — what was put in place, how it works in practice.
3. **Outcomes** — what concretely changed, ideally with numbers.

A case study is **not a "success story" and not a brochure**. A success story
argues from the vendor's seat and praises the product. The vendor's voice has
no place in the body text. Credibility comes from the customer's voice and
from verifiable facts, not from superlatives.

The customer is the hero of the story. The vendor is the partner who made the
journey possible.

---

## Audience and perspective

Case studies are read by **decision-makers at companies considering your
product**. They are professionally informed, know their own business well, and
are looking for answers to three questions:

1. Do I recognise my own company and my own problems in this story?
2. What real bottlenecks did this solution remove — and how?
3. Is this vendor a partner I can trust over years?

The leading perspective is whoever holds the budget and the accountability —
typically a managing director, COO, operations or IT lead. Day-to-day
operators may appear in the story because they make it concrete, but they are
not the addressee.

## Two types of case studies

The type determines the entire shape of the story. Decide it before you write
anything.

### Type 1 — New customer (Before / After)

**When:** The customer adopted the product recently (rule of thumb: in the
last one to three years). There is a clear, narratable break between *before*
and *after*.

**Heart of the story:** the contrast. The piece lives off making the *before*
tangible — spreadsheets, sticky notes, scattered data sources, manual
calculations, lack of visibility — and setting *today* against it. The more
concrete the before, the stronger the after lands.

**Arc:**
1. Company portrait
2. Starting point / the pain points before (in depth, with images)
3. Decision (why this vendor)
4. The solution / what concretely changed
5. Outcomes (verifiable numbers) and outlook

**What matters most:** verifiable before/after improvements. When the customer
mentions numbers ("stock count went from two days to one hour", "quotes drafted
in two minutes instead of fifteen", "inventory reduced by 25%"), they belong
prominently in the text.

### Type 2 — Long-term customer (Topic or module focus)

**When:** The customer has used the product for many years. A simple
before/after would feel forced — nobody vividly remembers "the time before",
and that is not the point. What matters here is depth, not rupture.

**Heart of the story:** one concrete topic, or a small set of modules, fully
illuminated — embedded in a partnership that has grown over years. Examples
of such topics: production planning, quality and traceability, business
analytics, multi-site operations, Industry 4.0, procurement automation.

**Arc:**
1. Company portrait (including "customer since [year]" — signals trust and
   continuity)
2. Context: the journey so far (how the partnership grew, what was built)
3. The current topic / challenge
4. The solution / modules in focus (concrete use cases)
5. Benefit and outlook (forward-looking)

**What matters most:** the partnership and the continuous evolution. The
system "grows with the company". Verifiable improvements are still valuable,
but they relate to the topic at hand, not to an overall rupture. A
before/after *within* the topic is allowed (e.g. "magnetic planning board →
digital planning"), but it stays subordinate to the topic.

### Which type is more common?

In our experience, Type 2 dominates. Most long-standing customers don't have
a clean before/after — they have a partnership and an evolving set of
problems. Do not reflexively construct a before/after narrative just because
it is the easier shape. With a long-term customer, that reads as fabricated.

> **Rule of thumb when in doubt:** "Customer since" less than ~3 years **and**
> a clear pre-system contrast in the source material → Type 1. Otherwise →
> Type 2. A long-standing customer with a genuine pivot (new ownership, major
> version jump, new site) can still carry a before/after — that is a bridge
> case, and the contrast then sits inside the larger continuity.

## Sources

The source material is not always a fresh interview. Common inputs:

- **Interview transcript** (e.g. recorded Teams or Zoom call) — the classic
  case.
- **Webinar recording** — the customer speaks for 40–60 minutes about their
  company. Different shape from an interview, see below.
- **The customer's website** — for company profile, industry, key figures,
  current focus areas.
- **Other material** the writer provides: briefing, earlier publications,
  press coverage.

### Decide your sources up front

Before drafting, agree on which sources are in scope:

- The uploaded document only (interview or webinar), or
- Plus the customer's website for context and current focus, or
- Plus other sources you name explicitly.

This matters because of the truth rule below.

### The truth rule

**Anything not in the released sources is not claimed.** No general industry
knowledge is silently imported. Nothing is invented. If an important detail is
missing, mark it as `[NEEDS INPUT: what's missing]` and keep going. Do not
paper over gaps with plausible-sounding filler.

This is the single rule that separates a credible case study from marketing
fiction.

---

## Reading the source material

### Interview transcript

Raw transcripts (Teams, Zoom, Otter) are messy. Work through them like this:

- **Separate speakers.** Distinguish the interviewer (your side, asking
  questions) from the customer (their side, providing substance). Only the
  customer's content is case study material. The questions give you topics
  and structure.
- **Spoken language → written.** Remove filler, false starts, dialect, broken
  sentences. Carry the meaning into clean prose without changing it.
- **Multiple speakers on the customer side.** Attribute every statement to the
  right person. This matters for quote attribution.
- **Topics jump.** Interviews meander. Re-order the substance into the arc
  appropriate to the type. The transcript order is not the report order.

### Webinar recording

A webinar is not an interview and needs different handling:

- **Monologue, not Q&A.** The customer often speaks at length without targeted
  follow-ups. Content is less sharp and mixes with presentation and product
  demo. Filter for the case-study-relevant moments yourself.
- **Promotional register.** Webinars tend to be more positive and general.
  Distil the **concrete, verifiable** statements; drop generic praise.
- **More context, less focus.** A webinar often covers the whole company.
  Choose the thread that fits the type and the strongest topic.
- **Strip out the host / moderator.** Only what the customer says is
  substance.
- **Quotes are rarely pointed.** You will often have to work zone-by-zone to
  find a quotable statement. If nothing clean is on tape, mark it as
  `[EDITORIAL: a strong quote on partnership would land here]` rather than
  inventing one.

### Customer website (when in scope)

Used for: company profile, industry, key figures, self-description, current
focus areas (new products, locations, awards) that ground the story. Same
truth rule applies: only carry over what actually appears there.

## Building blocks (extract before writing)

Before drafting, pull these eight blocks out of the sources. If one is
missing, mark it `[NEEDS INPUT: …]`.

1. **Company profile** — name, legal form, industry / core product, sites,
   headcount, founding year, customer since, number of users.
2. **Interview partner(s)** — full name plus role (required for quote
   attribution).
3. **Leading topic** — what is this really about? (Type 1: the change
   overall. Type 2: the concrete topic or module.)
4. **Starting point / pain points** — what was hard before? (Type 2: what was
   the challenge before the focus module came in?)
5. **Solution** — which modules or functions are named? How are they used in
   practice?
6. **Verifiable outcomes** — numbers, time savings, concrete improvements
   that **actually appear in the sources**.
7. **Quotable statements** — places where the customer is pointed, vivid, or
   especially clear. Candidates for the two to four quotes, in particular the
   pull quote.
8. **Outlook** — next steps, planned developments.

The W-questions (who, what, why, how, when, where) are a good completeness
check.

## Make improvements concrete

The single most persuasive thing in a case study is a **specific, verifiable
improvement**. Surface it; don't bury it in general statements.

- **Prefer measurable before/after values.** "Stock count from two days to
  one hour." "Quote turnaround from fifteen minutes to two." "Inventory
  reduced by around 25%." "Planning runs overnight; data ready in the
  morning."
- **Format:** with a concrete unit (minutes, hours, days, percent, count).
  A number lands harder than an adjective.
- **Placement:** in the running text at the right spot, and additionally — if
  appropriate — in the figures box.
- **Hard line:** these numbers are **never invented or inflated**. They must
  appear in the source. If no concrete number is available, describe the
  improvement qualitatively and honestly ("noticeably faster" only if the
  customer says so), or flag with `[EDITORIAL: a concrete time / volume
  figure would land here, if available]`.

## Voice and tone

Case studies are **professional but alive** — good reportage, not a leaflet.
Imagine an experienced colleague telling a peer what changed for the customer.

### Ground rules

- **Third person.** Write about the customer in the third person. Never "we"
  from the vendor's perspective.
- **Active voice.** "Northvale plans on a rolling 12-week horizon" — not
  "At Northvale, planning is performed on a rolling 12-week horizon".
- **Concrete verbs.** Shortens, saves, documents, plans, connects, spots.
  Avoid abstract hollows: enables, ensures, guarantees.
- **Avoid nominal style.** "The reduction of manual activities" → "Data no
  longer needs to be entered twice."
- **Mix sentence lengths.** Short and medium sentences. Avoid nested
  constructions.
- **Use industry vocabulary as a credibility anchor.** Concrete domain terms
  from the customer's world (cleanroom, ADR for hazardous goods, traceability,
  multi-machine operation) show that the piece understands the business. Use
  sparingly and correctly.
- **Punctuation:** prefer colons and full stops. Em dashes and semicolons
  sparingly.

### Taboo words in the body text

These words and their cousins are **only allowed when an immediate concrete
effect is shown alongside**. Without evidence, strike them and replace with
something specific:

> efficient / efficiency · seamless · holistic / comprehensive · innovative /
> smart / intelligent · future-proof · powerful · optimise / optimisation ·
> user-friendly / intuitive · maximise / minimise (as ends in themselves) ·
> significant / considerable / substantial (as empty intensifiers) · flexible
> (without saying what is flexed and against what)

**Exception — verbatim customer quotes.** Inside a quote, the customer's
language is left alone. If the managing director says "more transparent,
more efficient, more flexible", it is quoted, not smoothed. Authenticity
inside quotation marks beats style rules.

### Weak vs. strong

| Weak (vague)                       | Strong (concrete, evidenced)                                              |
| ---------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| "more efficiency"                  | "orders now come together in one place"                                   |
| "seamlessly integrated"            | "feeds directly into the finance system"                                  |
| "full transparency"                | "you can see at a glance where an order is"                               |
| "optimised processes"              | "stock count takes an hour instead of two days"                           |
| "powerful solution"                | "planning runs overnight; data is ready in the morning"                   |

**Weak:** The ERP brings more efficiency and transparency to all processes.
**Strong:** Sticky notes and spreadsheets are gone. Information runs straight
into the system; purchasing pulls bills of materials and stock levels and
calculates requirements without detours.

## Craft moves that lift a draft from "fine" to "convincing"

1. **Open with a scene or a tension.** The first sentence either paints the
   company vividly or names a real friction. Not "Founded in …".
2. **One thing per paragraph.** Each paragraph makes exactly one point. Keeps
   the piece walkable.
3. **Alternate prose and direct speech.** Narration and quotes in rotation —
   gives rhythm and credibility.
4. **Put the person on stage.** Introduce the interview partner with name and
   role; give them a literal voice. People convince people.
5. **Lead with the strongest number.** If you have a striking, verified
   improvement, don't bury it in the last paragraph.
6. **Honesty over polish.** Setbacks may appear if the customer mentions them
   ("acceptance was rocky at first", "migration took longer than expected").
   That makes the piece more credible than a pure success report.
7. **Soft transitions.** Link paragraphs logically (starting point →
   decision → solution → outcome). Don't just stack them.
8. **Hold a single thread.** Title, subline, opening and closing all pick up
   the same core theme.
9. **Never name a module in isolation.** Pair every function with its effect
   in daily work, never as a bare list.
10. **End forward-looking.** Close on how the journey continues, usually with
    the strongest quote.

## From spoken word to quote (quote refinement)

Quotes are the centre of gravity. They **always come from the sources**, but
may be lightly polished for readability.

**Allowed:** remove filler and false starts, smooth syntax, cut repetitions,
carry dialect into standard language.
**Not allowed:** change the meaning, sharpen it, soften it, or insert words
that were never said.

**Example:**
- Raw: "Um, yeah, I'd do it again, the system kind of grows with us, you
  know, and they actually think along with us, they don't just sell."
- Refined: "I would choose this system again any time. It grows with the
  company, and we have a partner that thinks alongside us."

Every quote is attributed with **full name, role, and company**. Anonymous
quotes are not allowed.

- **Right:** "We needed a partner who thinks ahead with us — that is exactly
  what we get." — *Eleanor Mahesh, Managing Director, Northvale Brewing
  Equipment Ltd.*
- **Wrong:** "A managing director reports significant improvements."

**Pull quote:** exactly one especially pointed statement (max. two sentences),
set apart prominently — usually about the partnership or the overall benefit.

## Structure

### Title and subline

- **Headline:** short, with rhythm or a small wordplay; picks up the core
  theme. Avoid adjective triplets ("Fast. Flexible. Secure.") and buzzword
  chains.
- **Subline:** more specific, often names the customer.

### Section headings — deliberately not fixed

Section headings are not templated. They are written individually per piece,
content-specific, often image-led. Aim for four to six headings.

### Order, by type

- **Type 1:** Portrait → starting point / pain → decision → solution →
  outcomes and outlook.
- **Type 2:** Portrait (+ customer since year) → journey so far → current
  topic → modules in focus → benefit and outlook.

### Opening / company portrait

Image-led opening, then industry / core product, headcount, location, and (for
Type 2) "customer since". Four to eight sentences.

### Closing

Forward-looking. Often ends on the strongest quote or a reaffirmation of the
partnership.

### Length

- **Body text total: roughly 600–900 words** (about four content pages plus
  cover and figures box, in a typical print layout).
- Paragraphs: three to five sentences.
- No bullet lists in the body text (one exception: the figures box).

## Handling missing information

When a needed detail is missing, **do not invent it.** Two markers:

- `[NEEDS INPUT: what's missing]` — for a fact you don't have but need
  (e.g. `[NEEDS INPUT: headcount]`).
- `[EDITORIAL: …]` — for a place where a quote or figure would clearly
  strengthen the piece, if available.

At the end of the draft, list all markers so the editor can plug them
specifically.

### Ask first, or mark inline?

- **Ask first** (before drafting) if the missing piece **shapes the whole
  piece**: unclear type, missing customer name, unclear leading topic. A
  draft built on those gaps is wasted work.
- **Mark inline** if it's a **single detail** that doesn't block the
  structure: headcount, user count, year, exact module name, one specific
  figure. Keep writing; flag the gap.

Rule of thumb: if the gap blocks the shape → ask. If it's a field → mark.

## Customer approval

A case study is **published externally**. Statements, and especially verbatim
quotes, must be approved by the customer before publication.

- The delivered draft is always a **draft**, not the final publishable
  version.
- End every draft with the line:
  **"Draft — quotes and statements to be approved by the customer before
  publication."**
- Quotes are formatted so the customer can confirm or adjust them easily
  (clearly marked as quotes, with name, role, and company).
- Anything sensitive about people, the company, or third parties (numbers
  with competitive bearing, internal politics, critical statements) is
  flagged as approval-required, not slipped in.

---

## Correct module and product names

Module and product names are **never guessed**. Source order:

1. **Your module glossary** (see `examples/module-glossary.example.md` for the
   shape) — the binding list of names and spellings.
2. **The source itself** — if the name appears verbatim in interview, webinar,
   or website.
3. **The vendor's website** — for the current official spelling.
4. Only if none of the above is unambiguous: describe the function neutrally
   and mark `[NEEDS INPUT: correct module name?]`.

Wrong or invented module names are the same mistake as invented numbers.

## Worked example: from source to paragraph

**Raw transcript excerpt:**
> *Interviewer 0:14:20:* And how was that before, before the system?
> *E. Mahesh 0:14:25:* Yeah so we had everything in Excel basically, and that
> was, well, every production plan was a massive effort, and stock count, god,
> that used to take like two days normally, and now with the ERP, yeah, an
> hour or so. Pretty wild actually.

**Extracted:** pain (Excel, planning effort) · verified outcome (stock count
2 days → 1 hour) · quote candidate.

**Finished paragraph (narration + refined quote):**

> Before the new system went in, Northvale ran much of the business in Excel.
> Production planning was hand work. Stock count took two days. Today the
> system handles those jobs in a fraction of the time: stock count is down to
> around an hour. "I couldn't have pictured that a few years ago," says
> Managing Director Eleanor Mahesh.

The quote is only allowed when a substantively matching statement actually
appears in the source. Otherwise, narrate the verified fact and flag the
quote slot with `[EDITORIAL: …]`.

## What this method does *not* do

- Invent numbers, quotes, or examples.
- Describe images, or refer to figures that aren't in the body text.
- Force a long-term customer into a before/after shape.
- Write from the vendor's seat. The case study is from the customer's seat.

If the source material genuinely doesn't carry the weight of a publishable
piece, the right move is to go back and gather more — not to write around
the gap.
